• Thesis statement – sentence stating the overall point an argument is trying to make

    • Cannot be purely opinionated

      • Blue is the best color

      • Cake is better than fact

    • Cannot be pure fact

      • Tuesday comes after Monday

    • Shouldn't be self-evidently true

      • Will require evidence to support

      • Should be arguable (should be able to argue against)

    • A “promise to your reader”

      • Limits the scope of the argument

  • “You have been acting like a jerk lately” - good thesis statement

    • Evidence can be presented in support of this statement

    • Someone can argue against it

    • Not self-evidently true

  • Three becauses

    • You don’t need to put your reasons in the thesis statement

      • “Tom Brady is the greatest NFL quarterback of all time because of his passing skills, his durability, and his ability to win big games.” - BAD

      • “Tom Brady is the greatest NFL quarterback of all time.” - GOOD

  • Using “Although” for thesis - “Although (negative to your claim), the (positive claim).”

    • Although the film Crazy Rich Asians regrettably upholds the “tiger mom” parenting stereotype, the film is a net positive in its dismissal of stereotypes of Asian women.

  • Topic Sentence – a sentence that summarizes the main idea of a body paragraph

    • Limits the scope of the paragraph

    • Should leave examples and other evidence out

  • Three earmarks of a good topic sentence (will be on final)

    • Announces unique topic of the paragraph

    • Clearly connects to the thesis

    • Uses transition words to show how the topic fits into the larger essay

      • “In addition,” or "Furthermore”

      • “Another way” or “An additional way”

      • “Also” or “You have also”

      • “One way”

  • Topic sentences must focus on one thing

    • Topic must be talked about only in this paragraph

  • TEA Paragraph

    • Topic Sentence

    • Evidence

    • Analysis

    • Everything in the body paragraphs should be one of these three roles
