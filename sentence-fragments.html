Sentence Fragments

ENGL 100

Niko Kompella

 

  • Fragment – part of a sentence, not a complete sentence
    • Missing subject or main verb
    • Pretends to be a sentence
    • Most common type of fragment is one that is standing next to an independent clause
      • “I left the party early. Because I was tired.”
  • We write in fragments because we talk in fragments
    • Particularly in response to a question
  • Independent Clause – a group of words that by themselves can be a sentence (will be on final)
    • Can have multiple independent clauses
      • Because I skipped breakfast, I am hungry, so I will eat an early lunch.
    • Every complete sentence has an independent clause
  • Dependent Clause – cannot by itself be a sentence
    • Needs to attach itself to a sentence that has at least one independent clause
    • Also called subordinate clause
  • Why a fragment won’t be a sentence
    • No verb for the subject to do
      • “Three friends and I.”
    • No subject to do the verb
      • “Went to the store.”
    • A good independent clause is ruined by a subordinate conjunction
      • “Three friends and I went to the store.” - GOOD SENTENCE
      • “Because three friends and I went to the store” - BAD SENTENCE
  • Subordinate conjunction – when placed in front of an independent clause the clause becomes a dependent clause, and therefore a fragment
    • Because, while, since, when, even though, although
    • “I was tired”
      • “Because I was tired”
      • “While I was tired”
      • “Since I was tired”
      • “Although I was tired”
  • How to fix a fragment:
    • Attach it to an existing independent clause
      • “I left the party early, because I was tired.”
    • Rewrite it so it is now independent
  • Participial phrase – dependent clause that is attached to an independent clause
    • “Wishing to avoid an unpleasant scene.” - Participial phrase
      • “Wishing to avoid an unpleasant scene, I left the room.”
    • Be careful starting a sentence with a word ending in “-ing”
  • Use a colon to introduce a list only if it follows an independent clause
  • Although and which
    • Although is a subordinate conjunction
      • Any sentence starting with although must eventually come to a comma followed by an independent clause
    • Which is a subordinate conjunction
      • Don’t start a sentence with which unless it is a question
